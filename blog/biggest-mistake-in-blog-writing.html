Rambling on about the biggest issues that plague the world of blogging: grammar, spelling, and punctuation.

March 27, 2015

Your going to like this one.

If you found nothing wrong with the above fragment, please consider taking another look and revising your opinion. What I'm here to talk about today is punctuation, grammar, and spelling errors in blog posts, and how they really just come down to laziness.

Proofreading is an essential part of being an online blogger, journalist, reviewer, or what have you. Not only can it save the opinions of your readers about what you wrote and you as a writer, but it also gives you a chance to revisit what you've written. There's no shame in revising something that you had considered finished.

Online Tools

In the digital age (which, by the way, is now) where tools to improve your own shortcomings are in abundance, there's no excuse for making simple mistakes such as you're / your and there / their / they're.

Websites such as grammarly.com offer free (with registration, mind you) grammar, sentence structure, punctuation, and spelling edits to your uploaded text. It's as simple as taking your prepared blog post or news article and uploading the file or plain text to the site and clicking "Check your text".

Of course, you should have proofread the post several times yourself (over a number of days for best results) and given it to a colleague or friend to check over before using an online tool. Selecting an Online Tool

Consequences

You may be wondering how this all relates to writing blogs. (If you're seriously wondering that, please seek professional help.) This likely won't come as a surprise to regular bloggers or article writers, but this topic relates directly to your blog or news site.

I'm going to venture to guess that I'm not alone in saying that I will without a doubt become uninterested in an article if I find too many mistakes within it. Here are some negative outcomes of having a few mistakes in your writing:

You take the reader out of the experience.

By allowing spelling, punctuation, or grammar to get in the way of your article , you risk prohibiting your reader to do exactly what they clicked on your article to do. When a reader comes face-to-face with a mistake, they are taken out of the experience of enjoying your article. It's as if you're physically poking the user in the side while they attempt to read your content.

You put your brand in a negative light.

Each time a reader comes across a mistake, a little shred of your credibility is lost.

"Why did they not proofread their content?"

"Don't they care about what they post?"

You can lose a subscriber.

Depending on how forgiving your reader is, coming across little mistakes where a letter is left off a word or a couple letters are jumbled up may not necessarily get you one less subscriber.

Avoiding Mistakes

Avoiding mistakes is a straightforward process. You should be doing all that you can to get your post proofread by friends and colleagues before it's released to the public.

On top of that, use an online tool or piece of software to help you out. You shouldn't rely on tools or software alone, but they may pick up something your sentient help did not.

Closing Remarks

Whether you're writing a blog post about your adorable cat or your pet rock, make sure that your don't burden your readers with atrocious punctuation, grammar and spelling mistakes.

Though, if you absolutely need to write a train wreck of a sentence or two, I would recommend you convince your subconscious to do it in the middle of the post.
