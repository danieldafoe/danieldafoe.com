Usability.gov

If you're thinking what I'm thinking, then you know what this means. Yes, a website that provides information on UX has an issue with the very thing it claims to understand.

Closeby is a screenshot of the side navigation that appears on the usability.gov website. As you can see, it uses only icons to convey the meaning of each box that you can click on. (Who knew?)

Let's take it from the top. The top icon screams git repo to me, but that seems unlikely. A second guess would be that it's related to making connections or getting further details.

Alas, it has nothing to do with any of my educated guesses. "Methods" text appears when the icon is hovered over, which - of course - further confuses me. Are these methods of addressing usability? Methods of madness?

Clicking the icon/text brought me to a page with a title, two icons side-by-side, and no description of what I was looking at. So...am I just, like, supposed to click around? (The 'x' for this tab looks appealing.)

Informing the user

If your goal is to have users interact with something on your site, then it's wise to let them know that they can. You can use appropriate icons; you can use descriptive text; hell, you can even use CSS animations to point an arrow at what you want them to do. Above all, let them know where they can find what they're looking for. Usability.gov does a great job demonstrating what not to do:

With no text to describe what the purpose of this page is or what information you can find, users are left guessing. It's unlikely a typical user will even know that the icons under the title toggle the view-type of the boxes.
