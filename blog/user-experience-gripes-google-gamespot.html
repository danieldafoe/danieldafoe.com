User Experience Gripes: Google and Gamespot

Analyzing questionable user experience design decisions and how they impact the user.

July 1, 2015

Welcome to the first installment of User Experience Gripes. This is a compilation (however small it may be) of user experience oddities that I have picked up around the web during my usual browsing experience. Although the list may seem short for installment numero uno, don't fret! There is always more to question.

Without further ado, let's begin with the first culprit.

Google

The King of Search is great at building emotionally engaging web experiences and I've always truly valued their work in the world of web design. Although Google has been scorned before by some of the web community for their use of "scrolljacking" - whereby a website attempts to control the speed and fluency at which you can scroll - I've picked something different to discuss.

Below is a screenshot taken from Google's Polymer Starter Kit page, which is still live as of today. If you analyze the icon with the titles of the selling points of Polymer, you may notice that the icons are a bit ambiguous.

White diamond

If you direct your attention to the first benefit in the screenshot, "Components for nearly any app, out of the box", you can see what could have been a box in an alternate life...if that life were 45 degrees to the left or right. The only way I can possibly see this icon being justifiable is if it were rotated to look like a box instead of a diamond. Even then I expect a little more from a company like Google. In under 30 seconds I can imagine further additions such as a box with an open lid and confetti/items coming out of it.

Regardless of what it could be, it isn't what it should be: able to convey - at a glance - its relationship to its content. The content and the icon should be one cohesive unit, both delivering (with one reinforcing the other) a single message. When scanning the page, seeing that icon only causes confusion.

White chevron

"Framework-free, or framework-compatible". This icon choice seems a little out of nowhere. Unless the chevron pointing up is supposed to correspond with the last line of text, "It's up to you," I'm not sure I get the relationship between the two. I'm unsure of what relationship, if any, the chevron has to frameworks. I consulted Wikipedia in case I was missing out on some greater connection, but, alas, I was not.

Consulting with a friend in the area of information science, she brings up the impact of these choices on cognitive burden,

Icons that are not universally recognizable naturally increase cognitive burden and are typically nothing more than wasted space on a webpage, but can be justified if they fit the cultural semantics of the target user group. These icons increase cognitive burden two-fold by being neither universal nor recognizable to a particular user group given Google's international audience. They are extremely problematic.

Confusion ensues again for our dear user, while the rest of us try to piece together Google's thought process.

Gamespot

Offender number 2 is a company that reports on video game news, writes video game reviews, and offers previews of video games. Their presence is restricted to online, so you'd hope they make a point of concerning themselves with the experience they give their users. I won't say that they don't, but this hiccup is a questionable offense, and one that could have been avoided by more extensive research or user testing.

User Testing

To test the understanding of the toggle button's implied function, I conducted a small experiment. I would show images of the toggle to a group of people ask them what they thought it did. To ensure I didn't influence any opinions, I asked the question and supplied a screenshot over Facebook Messenger and gave only a few concise sentences to explain the button's environment.

I held two tests: one where only the button is shown on its own, and the next showing the button in its location on the web page. Here's the first image of just the toggle button,

and here are the results:

  • "Audio? The 'a' could be for audio."
  • "Automatic? Hmm. Uppercase to lowercase?"
  • "Is it alphabetized sorting?"
  • "I don't know. Some sort of quiz and you switch the slider to choose an answer?"
  • "Maybe its a slider that lets you choose between two options! One of them being option A!"

After gathering the initial responses without showing where on the site the toggle was located, it's obvious that no one had any clue. The toggle's design is clearly not universal enough to be understood and is therefore useless until a user interacts with it. However, knowing that clicks are spent sparingly by users means that an interaction may never come anyway.

Naturally, I asked the same people again, but this time showing them where the toggle was located on the web page. Listed below is the second image,

and here was the follow-up response:

  • "Audio. That's all I can think for 'A'."
  • "Font size?"
  • "Font size."
  • "Some sort of like...anti-spoiler button?"
  • "Alternate site view?"

It's interesting to look at just how scattered the guesses were for the experiment. This is telling of how universally unrecognizable the toggle button design is, and just how uncertain users become when they come across something online that isn't what they expect--or rather, what they've come to expect.

By the way, the correct answer was day and night/light and dark mode. Clicking the toggle gives an alternate view of the site by changing the background and text colours for an easier reading experience depending on your environment or preference. Examples of the change are inserted below,

Had Gamespot thought a little more about this, they may have come to the conclusion that there are better choices to denote a light and dark mode. How about a sun and moon? How about including some sort of label such as "Reading mode" above the toggle? How about a light switch? As you can see, there were definitely alternatives.

Conclusion

In closing, not even the most powerful companies or established news sites are perfect when user experience is concerned. You can't ignore the experience that users have when viewing pages on your domain, and you cannot overlook the importance of how users will interact with the pages you present to them.
